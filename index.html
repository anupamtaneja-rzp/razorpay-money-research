What we learned
Seven Insights

Click any insight to expand the evidence. Confidence levels reflect qual-quant alignment.

Insight 01HighQual: 6 of 6 assumed auto-apply at checkout

Users expect checkout "magic" — seamless checkout is table stakes

Action → Auto-detect RM balance at Razorpay checkout, one-click apply, zero codes. If this doesn't work on day one, nothing else matters.
▾
Qual: 6 of 6 assumed auto-apply at checkout

Every single interviewee assumed RM would work like a wallet — balance appears at Razorpay checkout, one-click apply, no codes. Nobody described a flow involving manual codes, copy-paste, or any extra steps. This isn't a feature to market — it's infrastructure to guarantee on day one. If checkout requires manual steps, all three ICPs lose trust immediately.

Everyone expects it to work like magic — just go to website, shop, Razorpay Money will be there at checkout.
Insight 02HighQual: 5 of 6 preferred Shopping · Quant: 28 chose Shopping vs 9 Exchange

Overwhelming preference for Shopping Voucher — launch with it alone

Action → Build Exchange later if demand emerges. Don't split focus at launch.
▾
Qual: 5 of 6 preferred Shopping · Quant: 28 chose Shopping vs 9 Exchange

Shopping Voucher wins across every segment. Zero interviewees chose Exchange cleanly when the concept was explained. Even rewards-driven users who already convert CC points to GCs prefer shopping directly rather than adding another conversion step. Exchange was misunderstood even with interviewer guidance — in a self-serve product, confusion will be higher.

I can get more value if I pay with credit card directly vs buying GC then using GC.
Insight 03HighQual: 6 of 6 conditioned interest on brands · Quant: 10 of 13 undecided blocked by brand uncertainty

Brand visibility before purchase is non-negotiable — what to show differs by ICP

Action → Landing page must lead with recognizable brand logos, not discount messaging. Tailor brand showcase by ICP segment.
▾
Qual: 6 of 6 conditioned interest on brands · Quant: 10 of 13 undecided blocked by "don't shop at niche brands"

Every interviewee conditioned their interest on seeing brands first. But what they need to see is different:

Insight 04HighQual: 3 of 7 raised explicitly · Strongest from Marketplace Migrators

Razorpay is trusted for payments — but not for shopping, delivery, or returns

Action → Consider easy refunds back to RM wallet. If a D2C order goes wrong, recovering RM balance (vs fighting a small brand's customer service) could bridge the Amazon trust gap.
▾
Qual: 3 of 7 raised explicitly · Strongest from Marketplace Migrators

Users trust Razorpay for payments — they see it at checkout everywhere. But payments trust doesn't transfer to shopping trust. Delivery, returns, and refunds are different kinds of trust, and the product currently solves only the first. Amazon's moat isn't price — it's the safety net of "I can always return it." RM checkout at a D2C brand provides payment certainty, not delivery certainty.

I trust Amazon more than D2C brands. Order tracking and notifications are much better on Amazon.
We can return it if we don't like it. Amazon makes sense, can always return.
Insight 05HighQual: 6 of 6 found 5-10% acceptable, not compelling · Quant: only 4 of 22 rejecters cited "low discount"

Flexibility and brand discovery drive RM interest — discount is just supporting proof

Action → Lead with what each ICP values — brand quality for Alchemists, familiarity for Migrators, simplicity for Newcomers. Use 5-10% discount as reinforcement, not the headline.
▾
Qual: 6 of 6 found 5-10% acceptable, not compelling · Quant: only 4 of 22 rejecters cited "low discount"

Users are drawn to RM for the ability to shop across 500+ brands with one balance — the flexibility and discovery of new brands is what excites them. The 5-10% discount is acceptable (below 5% feels trivial, above 20% triggers quality suspicion), but it's never the reason someone chooses RM. Among rejecters, "would rather pay directly" (9 of 22) and "don't shop at niche brands" (6 of 22) are far larger objections than "low discount" (4 of 22). The brand proposition is losing people, not the discount.

D2C checkout is more seamless, and I discover new brands.
Keep new ones in trend AND old stable ones.
Insight 06Moderate-HighQual: 4 of 6 had gifting as primary use · Quant: gifters convert at similar rates within each ICP

Gifting behaviors are distributed across all ICPs — it's a use case, not a persona

Action → Build gifting flow (recipient email, personal message, scheduled delivery) as P0. Corporate bulk gifting is a separate B2B opportunity.
▾
Qual: 4 of 6 had gifting as primary use · Quant: gifters convert at similar rates within each ICP

The original research treated gifters as a distinct low-converting segment (8% conversion). The data doesn't support that. When re-clustered by behavior, gifters appear across all three ICPs and convert at the same rate as self-use buyers within each segment. Gifting is a distribution channel: a Points Alchemist who gifts RM to 40 employees puts RM in 40 new hands. Self-use follows gifting, not the other way around.

Provides flexibility — team doesn't have to figure out how to use it.
Easy to send online via email — don't have to send it myself.
Insight 07ModerateQual: 3 of 7 habitual GC buyers showed strong interest · Quant: directional from niche experience data

Past niche GC experience predicts conversion better than GC habit alone

Action → First redemption is make-or-break. Launch with brands that have strong inventory and seamless checkout. A bad first transaction means permanent churn.
▾
Qual: 3 of 7 habitual GC buyers showed strong interest · Quant: directional from niche experience data

Points Alchemists (habitual GC buyers via CC rewards) convert strongest — 9 of 19 signed up (47%). But across all segments, past niche brand GC experience quality is the strongest single predictor. Respondents who tried niche brand GCs and had "no issues" show strong RM interest. Those who had "limited products/options" show near-zero interest. Same population, opposite outcomes — driven entirely by whether their last niche GC experience was good or bad.

Added friction of exploring different websites is a drop-off point.
Going deeper
ICP Deep Dive

Full behavioral profiles, brand requirements, and positioning guidance.

The Points Alchemist

Turns reward points into real value — monthly, systematically, across 3-6 credit cards.

Behaviors

  • Buy GCs regularly via CC reward points as a monthly/quarterly habit
  • Manage 3-6 credit cards optimized by category (fuel, dining, travel, UPI)
  • Treat GCs as a financial instrument, not a shopping decision
  • Not discount hunters — convenience and quality trump 5%
  • Some explore D2C via Instagram; others are brand-conservative but financially active
  • Benchmark RM against Gyftr, Amazon Pay, Smartbuy

Brand Requirements

  • Want breadth + depth — mix of trending D2C AND stable established brands
  • Specific brands named: Sony, Philips, Samsung, Apple, Adidas, Zara, H&M, Nykaa
  • Also: MyFitness, Muscle Blaze, Davidoff, Daily Objects, Pilgrim

Positioning

  • Lead with Shopping Voucher
  • Acquire via CC rewards ecosystem (banking apps, reward portals)
  • Brand quality is the convincer — don't lead with discount
  • Lead with "better than Amazon GC" (more brands, same ease)
Key risk: People with fully optimized rewards stacks may see RM as redundant rather than better.

The Marketplace Migrator

Shops 80-90% on Amazon. D2C is unfamiliar territory. Needs familiar logos to feel safe.

Behaviors

  • Buy GCs occasionally — weddings, family occasions, one-off self-use
  • Shop 80-90% on Amazon/Blinkit — transactional relationship with GCs
  • Need arises, buy Amazon GC, done — not exploratory

Brand Requirements

  • Want familiar everyday brands ONLY — NOT emerging D2C
  • Specific: Nike, Adidas, Reliance stores, Tata Chroma, PharmEasy
  • Also: MedPlus, BookMyShow, Cleartrip, Swiggy

Positioning

  • Lead with familiar brand logos on landing page
  • Wallet-first flow: park money, use later
  • Razorpay checkout as trust bridge
  • Heavy social proof: "10,000+ users," testimonials, money-back guarantee
Key risk: If launch catalog skews too niche/D2C, this segment bounces immediately.

The GC Newcomer

Never bought a gift card. Blank slate — no baggage, but also no mental model.

Behaviors

  • Default to cash/UPI for everything
  • No existing GC mental model
  • Avoidance reasons scattered: "impersonal," "never thought about it," "might expire," "cash is simpler"

Positioning

  • Avoid "gift card" language — use "shopping voucher"
  • Position as a new category: "Like cash, but 10% smarter"
  • Needs segmented messaging by sub-motivation

Research Gap

Zero qual depth — all 7 interviewees were existing GC buyers. Next research priority.

Key risk: Diverse sub-motivations + least evidence = hardest segment to message.

The Settled Shopper

Has decided how they shop and doesn't want an intermediary.

Why they reject RM

  • "Would rather pay directly at a brand I like" (41%)
  • "Don't shop at new age / niche brands" (27%)
  • "Low discount" (18%)
  • Structural objections — not solvable with better UX or trust signals

Recommendation

  • Don't pursue — ~1 in 3 respondents fall here
  • Accept as the addressable market ceiling

Projects We're Picking Up

Three initiatives directly informed by the research findings above.

Project 01

Invisible wallet load on first use

Users expect RM balance to just appear at checkout — nobody imagines a separate "load wallet" step. We're redesigning the FTUX so the first wallet load happens silently from the GC delivery email, matching the mental model research uncovered.

Insight 01 — Checkout magic
Project 02

Curated brand sections for discovery

Research shows our ICPs impulse-shop in specific categories — fashion, footwear, self-care, beauty & wellness. We're building curated sections that surface the right brands to the right users, driving both discovery and redemption.

Insight 03 & 05 — Brands & discovery
Project 03

ICP-driven positioning & landing pages

Each ICP needs different trust signals and value messaging — Alchemists care about brand quality, Migrators need familiar logos, Newcomers need a new mental model. We're redesigning the landing page and positioning to speak to each segment.

How we got here
Methodology

Quantitative

72 survey responses (cleaned). 18 email signups (25% conversion). Clustered by last GC purchase reason. Limitation: last purchase ≠ overall behavior. Sizing is directional.

Qualitative

7 in-depth interviews (30-45 min). Focused on past behavior, not hypotheticals. All quotes anonymized. Recruited only recent GC buyers.

ICP Construction

Anchored in qual behavioral patterns, sized with quant proxies. Qual and quant used different clustering axes — mapping is approximate.

High — Both sources agree
Moderate-High — Strong qual, directional quant
Moderate — One source clear, other directional
